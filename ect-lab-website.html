§ 00

Abstract

Classical complexity theory asks how hard it is to compute a function. Gödel's theorems ask what a formal system can prove. ECT asks a different question entirely: can a computational system formally verify that it knows a truth — when verifying that truth requires complete self-knowledge?

We conjecture the answer is no. Not always. And the boundary where this fails is non-empty, structurally rich, and observer-relative. ECT-1, ECT-2, and ECT-3 formalize this claim across three axes: incompleteness, irreversibility, and relativity.

§ 01

The Three Conjectures

ECT-1

Epistemic Incompleteness of Computation

For any sufficiently complex computational system, there exists a non-empty class of well-formed, decidable propositions it cannot verify — because verification requires a complete self-model whose computational cost exceeds the system's resource bound.

∃ U(S) ≠ ∅ : ∀P ∈ U(S), V_S(P) → requires Σ(S) cost(Σ(S)) > R(S) ⟹ V_S(P) unreachable for S
Open Conjecture
ECT-2

Epistemic Irreversibility

Every act of computation that produces a genuine epistemic update necessarily destroys prior epistemic content. Knowledge is not accumulated — it is transformed. And transformation has an irreversible cost.

∀C (non-trivial epistemic update): Δ(C) = E(S,t) \ E(S,t+1) ≠ ∅ ∄ C⁻¹ : cost(C⁻¹) ≤ cost(C) ∧ E(S,t) = recover(E(S,t+1), C⁻¹)
Open Conjecture
ECT-3

Observer Epistemic Relativity

Epistemic boundaries are not absolute. They are relative to the observer context of the system. Two computationally identical systems with different contexts will have non-overlapping unknowable sets. There is no view from nowhere in computation.

∀ S₁, S₂ : R(S₁) = R(S₂), O₁ ≠ O₂ ∂E(S₁, O₁) ≠ ∂E(S₂, O₂) [boundaries differ] ∂E(S₁, O₁) ∩ ∂E(S₂, O₂) ≠ ∅ [shared unknowables] ∂E(S₁, O₁) △ ∂E(S₂, O₂) ≠ ∅ [asymmetric unknowables]
Open Conjecture
§ 02

Core Definitions

Epistemic State
E(S, t)
The complete set of propositions that system S has verified as true at time t. The dynamic object ECT tracks across computations.
Epistemic Boundary
∂E(S)
The set of well-formed, decidable propositions that S cannot verify without achieving an unreachable complete self-model. The central object of ECT-1.
Complete Self-Model
Σ(S)
The total representation of S — its memory model, transition function, resource bound, and full epistemic state history. Computing Σ(S) is the central challenge of ECT-1.
Epistemic Collapse
The event when S attempts to verify a proposition requiring complete self-knowledge, and the attempt itself destroys the epistemic state needed to complete the verification. A computational paradox.
Observer Context
O = (P, F, Γ)
A tuple of prior beliefs P_prior, input filter F_input, and interaction history Γ that shapes a system's epistemic capabilities. The central object of ECT-3.
Epistemic Residue
Δ(C)
The set of propositions lost from the epistemic state after computation C is applied. Δ(C) = E(S,t) \ E(S,t+1). ECT-2 claims this is always non-empty.
§ 03

Unified Structure

Three faces. One structure.

ECT-1 establishes that the epistemic boundary exists. ECT-2 establishes that crossing it destroys what came before. ECT-3 establishes that its location depends on who is looking.

Together: every computational system has knowledge it cannot reach, loses knowledge it once had, and its particular pattern of unknowability is unique to its context.
